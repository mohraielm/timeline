History of Accessible Computing

Mid-1830s

Charles Babbage developed plans for the Analytical Engine, an early model of a digital computer. It featured punched cards for instructions, a memory unit to store numbers, sequential control for operations, and most of the other basic elements of the present-day computer.

1886

Herman Hollerith developed punch cards for the 1890 census, laying groundwork for data processing systems, leading to IBM.

1935

The American Foundation for the Blind published the first Talking Book Bulletin, promoting books on tape.

1972

The first national open-captioned program aired on PBS (The French Chef with Julia Child).

1972

Vinton Cerf developed early host-level protocols for ARPANET, a precursor to email, and communicated with his wife using text. It was the first network to implement the packet-switching technique, which breaks data into packets and sends them independently to their destination.

1975

Ray Kurzweil created the Kurzweil Reading Machine and OCR technology, and one of the first CCD scanners.

1977

Apple II computer was the first microprocessor disgned specifically for home use.

1986

Jim Thatcher created the IBM Screen Reader for DOS (Disk Operating System), an early tool that helped people with low vision use computers by reading out the text on the screen.

1990

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was passed, ensuring civil rights protections and accessibility for individuals with disabilities.

1995

Windows 95 included built-in accessibility features for the first time such as Sticky keys, FilterKeys, ToggleKeys, Mouse Keys,etc (we will see its improvement from this later in the timeline!).

1996

pwWebSpeak was introduced, providing users with a keyboard-based way to navigate web pages. It translated text into speech and supported large print display with customizable colors and sizes.

1997

NCR Corp developed the first audio ATM, accessible to blind or illiterate users.

1998

Synchronized Accessible Media Exchange (SAMI) was released to simplify captioning and audio description for videos.

1998

Section 508 was added to the Rehabilitation Act, mandating accessibility requirements for government websites.

1999

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) released Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG): important for later in the presentation :D.

2000

Windows 2000 was released with an on-screen keyboard and text-to-speech features.
